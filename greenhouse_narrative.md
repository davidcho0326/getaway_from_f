## 1. "Why do you want to work at Anthropic?" (Required, 200-400 words)

In 2016, I wrote a thesis on Augustine's *De Vera Religione* and concluded: the only path to truth is to build rigorously, believe, abandon that belief when evidence demands it, and rebuild. In 2023, I executed that thesis -- standing in front of 150,000 fashion images that no vendor could tag cost-effectively.

I tried manual labeling. Impossible at scale. Abandoned. I tried ChatGPT, pasting images one by one. Inconsistent. Abandoned. I tried Gemini structured I/O. Accuracy fell short. Abandoned. Then I built a Claude VLM API pipeline from first principles -- 90% accuracy, 90% cost reduction, five days. Three abandonments and one rebuild. The thesis, proved in production.

But it wasn't Claude's capability that drew me to Anthropic -- it was the constraint. When I built production MCP servers afterward (17 tools, 12-criterion quality validation, 11 auto-fail gates), I spent more time on guardrails than features. How do you prevent anatomically incorrect images from reaching marketing teams? How do you ensure brand safety in an automated pipeline? I realized that designing for what AI *shouldn't* do is exactly what Anthropic does at a civilizational scale. That alignment felt less like a career choice and more like recognition.

Over the past two years, I've rebuilt from scratch: 3 production MCP servers with 50+ tools, AI adoption for 320 employees across Korea and China, and a FNF Studio platform scaled from KRW 40M to 1B. But the work that convinced me to apply was something I started doing on my own: training startup C-level executives on Claude-based AI integration across 6 industries. Watching a CEO go from "how do I prompt this?" to "how do I connect MCP to our data pipeline?" taught me that the hardest barrier to AI adoption isn't technical. It's trust. And I discovered that education builds trust in ways pitching never can -- one engagement even converted into a commercial contract.

The thesis says: build, believe, abandon, rebuild. My career has been four iterations of this cycle. Each one brought me closer to Anthropic. Now I want to practice the method at the one company that takes safety as seriously as capability.

## 3. Additional Information (Optional — Cover Letter / Supplementary)

Three things my resume can't fully convey:

**Where it started**: Before my career began, every thread was already there. As a Samsung Dream Class mentor, I lived with and taught underprivileged children for a month -- learning that teaching is the deepest form of trust-building. As an intern at the Sejong Center for the Performing Arts, I sold arts program sponsorships to corporations -- my first experience selling intangible value to skeptical buyers. As philosophy department representative, I discovered that understanding people's needs and translating them into action was the work I wanted to do. These aren't biographical footnotes. They are the roots of everything I do now: the educator-led sales method, the instinct to cross domain boundaries, and the belief that the hardest things to sell create the most value.

**How I think about safety**: Every production system I've built includes deliberate constraints. The image generation platform has 12-criterion quality validation with 11 auto-fail gates -- because I learned that shipping AI without guardrails isn't just irresponsible, it's commercially unsustainable. When a generated image reaches a marketing team with a deformed hand, trust evaporates. This experience with AI safety at the product level is what I'd bring to conversations with startup CTOs.

**Why this pattern matters for Anthropic**: My thesis predicted a method -- build, believe, abandon, rebuild. My career proved it works across media, fashion, AI systems, and cross-border markets. The 4-stage tool evolution (manual to ChatGPT to Gemini to Claude) is the micro version of a macro career pattern. Anthropic's AE meets a different startup vertical every day and needs someone whose strength isn't depth in one domain, but the meta-capability to rapidly internalize any domain and deploy the right solution. That's not a talent -- it's a trained philosophical discipline. And it's why the method became the product: I now teach founders this same cycle, and the sale follows naturally.
